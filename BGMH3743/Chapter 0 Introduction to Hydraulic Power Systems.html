Introduction

1. Fluid Transport vs. Fluid Power

Fluid transport is a system that moves liquid from one location to another.
Fluid power is a system that moves energy from one location to another.

Fluid Transport
Air Selangor, Indah Water, water pumps

Fluid Power
hydraulic presses, excavators, stamping machines

2. Hydraulics vs. pneumatics

Feature Hydraulic Power Pneumatic Power
Medium Used Liquid (usually oil or water-based fluids) Gas (usually compressed air or nitrogen)
Power Output High power and force output Lower power and force output compared to hydraulics
Pressure Range Typically 1,000 – 5,000 psi (can go higher) Typically 80 – 120 psi
Speed Control Provides smooth and precise speed control Faster but less precise speed control
Response Time Slower response due to fluid inertia Faster response due to compressibility of air
Energy Efficiency More efficient; less energy loss Less efficient due to air compression losses
Compressibility Incompressible fluid allows precise motion control Air is compressible, leading to spring-like movement
System Components Uses pumps, hydraulic cylinders, valves, reservoirs, and hoses Uses compressors, air cylinders, valves, filters, and tubing
Maintenance Requires regular fluid checks and contamination control Easier maintenance but requires dry, clean air
Cost Higher initial cost but lower operational cost over time Lower initial cost but higher operating cost due to air compression inefficiency
Suitability Ideal for heavy-duty applications (construction, aircraft, industrial presses) Ideal for light-duty applications (assembly lines, robotics, air tools)
Safety Risk of fluid leaks causing hazards; high pressure can be dangerous Generally safer but still has risks of pressure bursts
Mobility Less portable due to heavy components and fluid reservoirs More portable due to lightweight components and air supply flexibility
Environmental Impact Hydraulic oil can cause pollution if not managed properly Environmentally friendly as air is used and exhausted

3. Hydraulics vs. other power systems

Hydraulics has good power density i.e. the amount of power generated per unit volume.
Other power systems e.g. electric motors, mechanical linkage have other advantages and disadvantages.

Case Study #1

A company is designing a six-axis industrial robotic arm for precision assembly tasks in an automotive manufacturing plant. The system must handle medium payloads (~10 kg), operate with high precision, and function efficiently in an automated production line.

Case Study #2

A manufacturing plant requires a continuous conveyor belt system to transport raw materials between production stations. The system must be robust, low-maintenance, and energy-efficient while handling loads up to 500 kg per cycle.

Case Study #3

A factory is planning to install a press machine for metal stamping and forming operations. The machine must generate high force (50 tonnes), operate reliably, and handle heavy-duty cycles. The company is evaluating hydraulic, mechanical, and electrical power systems to determine the most suitable option.

Appendix

Case Study 1

Step 1: Identifying Key Requirements
The robotic arm must:

  • Provide smooth and precise motion control.
  • Have high-speed operation with low inertia.
  • Ensure repeatability and accuracy (±0.02 mm).
  • Be energy-efficient for continuous 24/7 operation.
  • Have a compact and lightweight structure.
  • Require minimal maintenance.

Step 2: Evaluating Power System Options

Criteria Hydraulic Power Electrical Power Mechanical Power
Precision Low (fluid compressibility affects control) High (servo motors allow precise control) Moderate (backlash in gears may reduce precision)
Speed Moderate (hydraulic actuators have slower response times) High (servo motors have fast response and acceleration) Moderate (depends on gear ratio and inertia)
Power Density Very high (handles heavy loads well) Moderate (depends on motor size) Low (requires large mechanical components)
Energy Efficiency Moderate (fluid losses and heat generation) High (electric motors have minimal losses) High (direct power transmission is efficient)
Maintenance High (requires fluid management and leak control) Low (servo motors require minimal maintenance) Moderate (mechanical wear and tear over time)
Compactness Requires reservoirs and hoses, making it bulky Compact (motors and drives are integrated easily) Bulky (requires gears, pulleys, and linkages)
Environmental Impact Leaks can be hazardous (hydraulic oil spills) Environmentally friendly Environmentally friendly
Application Suitability Best for heavy-duty tasks (e.g., construction, foundries) Best for precision automation (e.g., robotic arms, CNC machines) Best for simple linkages (e.g., conveyor belts, manual tools)

Step 3: Selection Justification
Given the above evaluation, the best choice for this precision robotic arm is Electrical Power (Servo Motors) due to:
✅ High precision – Servo motors provide accurate motion control.
✅ Fast response time – Essential for high-speed assembly lines.
✅ Energy efficiency – Reduces operating costs.
✅ Low maintenance – No fluid leaks or complex lubrication.
✅ Compact design – Allows lightweight and modular construction.

Step 4: Implementation Strategy

  • Brushless AC servo motors for each axis to provide smooth and precise movement.
  • Harmonic drive gears to reduce backlash and enhance positioning accuracy.
  • An electronic control system (PLC or industrial PC) for seamless integration into an automated production line.
  • Sensors (encoders, torque feedback) for adaptive motion control and safety.

Conclusion
For this mechatronics application, electrical power (servo motors) is the best choice due to its high precision, efficiency, compactness, and low maintenance. Hydraulics would be excessive for a medium-load robotic arm, and mechanical linkages alone would lack the required flexibility and precision.

Case Study 2

Step 1: Identifying Key Requirements
The conveyor belt system must:

  • Provide continuous, smooth motion.
  • Handle heavy loads without excessive wear.
  • Operate with minimal maintenance over long hours.
  • Be energy-efficient with direct power transmission.
  • Have a long service life.

Step 2: Evaluating Power System Options

Criteria Mechanical Power Hydraulic Power Electrical Power
Speed Moderate (consistent speed using gear ratios) Low (hydraulic actuators have slower response) High (variable speed control possible)
Precision Moderate (suitable for simple motion like conveyor systems) Low (fluid compressibility affects accuracy) High (servo motors allow precise control)
Power Density Moderate (depends on motor and gearbox) Very high (can handle heavy loads) Moderate (depends on motor size)
Maintenance Low (requires lubrication but minimal upkeep) High (fluid management and leak prevention) Low (motor servicing required periodically)
Energy Efficiency High (minimal transmission losses in direct mechanical drive) Moderate (fluid losses and heat dissipation) Moderate (electrical resistance and motor efficiency losses)
Durability Very High (mechanical linkages last for years with proper lubrication) High (robust but prone to leaks) Moderate (motor bearings and drives may wear out)
Cost Lowest (simple gears and belts) High (complex pumps and valves) Moderate (electric motor cost)
Application Suitability Best for simple motion transmission (e.g., conveyor belts, pulley systems) Best for high-force applications (e.g., heavy presses) Best for automated systems (e.g., robotic arms)

Step 3: Selection Justification
A mechanical power system (geared motor with belt drive) is the best choice because:
✅ Simple and cost-effective – No need for complex hydraulics or servo motors.
✅ Energy-efficient – No fluid losses, only minimal friction losses.
✅ Low maintenance – Requires only occasional lubrication.
✅ Long lifespan – Gear systems and belts can last years with proper care.

Step 4: Implementation Strategy

  • Use a 3-phase induction motor with a gearbox to drive the conveyor.
  • Employ belt or chain drive to transmit motion efficiently.
  • Add roller bearings to reduce friction and wear.
  • Integrate a simple control panel with speed adjustment if needed.

Conclusion

A mechanical power system (gear-driven conveyor) is ideal for this application due to low cost, durability, and energy efficiency. Hydraulics would be excessive, and electric servo motors would be unnecessary for continuous, non-variable motion.

Case Study 3

Step 1: Identifying Key Requirements
The press machine must:

  • Generate very high force to deform metal sheets.
  • Operate with consistent force output under high loads.
  • Have reasonable energy efficiency for long operating hours.
  • Require minimal maintenance and high durability.
  • Provide control over pressing speed and pressure.

Step 2: Evaluating Power System Options

Criteria Hydraulic Power Mechanical Power Electrical Power
Speed Moderate – Slower cycle times due to fluid compression. High – Rapid cycle times with flywheel momentum. High – Electric presses can have fast actuation.
Precision & Control Good – Adjustable pressure, but response time is slower. Moderate – Fixed stroke length, harder to adjust. Excellent – Servo presses allow programmable force and position control.
Maintenance Moderate to High – Requires fluid changes and leak management. Low – Simple mechanical parts, fewer failure points. Low – Requires motor servicing, but no fluid leaks.
Force Output Very high – Suitable for heavy-duty metal stamping. Very high – Uses flywheels and crank mechanisms for stamping force. Moderate – Limited by motor torque.
Energy Efficiency Moderate – Hydraulic pumps consume power even when idle. High – Uses direct mechanical energy, no idle losses. High – Efficient motor-to-motion conversion.
Durability High – Designed for heavy loads, but fluid-related wear occurs. High – Mechanical components last a long time with lubrication. Moderate – Motors and electronics degrade over time.
Cost (Initial & Operational) Moderate-High – Higher initial cost, but adjustable force provides flexibility. Low-Moderate – Lower initial cost but may require larger footprint. High – Expensive upfront due to servo motors and controllers.
Application Suitability Best for heavy-duty, high-force applications. Best for high-speed stamping operations. Best for precision forming and light to medium loads.

Step 3: Selection Justification
A hydraulic power system (hydraulic press with adjustable pressure control) is the best choice because:
✅ High force output – Essential for forming thick metal sheets and deep drawing applications.
✅ Adjustable pressure – Unlike mechanical presses, force can be varied to suit different materials and thicknesses.
✅ Controlled motion – Smooth, steady pressing motion without sudden impact, reducing material stress.
✅ Compact design – Hydraulic presses require less floor space than equivalent mechanical presses.
✅ Durability under heavy loads – Hydraulic cylinders handle extreme pressure cycles with consistent performance.

Step 4: Implementation Strategy

  • Use a hydraulic cylinder with an adjustable pressure pump.
  • Implement a hydraulic accumulator to improve response time and energy efficiency.
  • Integrate a PLC or CNC controller for force and cycle control.
  • Install a cooling system to prevent overheating of hydraulic fluid.
  • Design a safety mechanism (pressure relief valves, emergency stop).
