Chapter 4: Hydraulic Pumps and Actuators

Hydraulic pumps and actuators are identical in working principle and very similar in construction. Under certain conditions, you could even use some designs of pumps as motors and vice versa.

Dynamic pumps like aquarium circulation pumps or flood mitigation pumps work by imparting kinetic energy to the fluid. Positive displacement pumps, also known as static pumps, function by moving small volumes of liquid from the inlet to the outlet. This displacement motion can be rotational or reciprocating.

Since the action of the static pump is independent of system conditions downstream, the maximum pressure a positive displacement pump can generate depends on which component in the entire system fails first.

4.1.3 Vane pumps

Vane pumps consist of a cam ring containing a slotted hub with vanes sitting inside. As the hub, or rotor, spins, springs and centrifugal force pushes the vanes against the housing, creating a sealed chamber. The hub is offset from the housing, creating eccentric circles. This offset creates chambers that expand and contract, creating suction and discharge.

In some designs of vane motor, the eccentricity can be adjusted, changing the difference between the largest and smallest chambers. This creates a variable displacement vane pump.

The eccentricity, eeee, is given as:

e=\frac{D_C-D_R}{2}
e=DC−DR2e=\frac{D_C-D_R}{2}e=2DC​−DR​​

where D_CDCD_CDC​ is the cam diameter and D_RDRD_RDR​ is the rotor diameter.

Similarly to the gear pump, the volumetric displacement of the pump starts with:

V_D=\frac{\pi}{4}(D_C^2-D_R^2)L
VD=π4(DC2−DR2)LV_D=\frac{\pi}{4}(D_C^2-D_R^2)LVD​=4π​(DC2​−DR2​)L

expanding the brackets,

V_D=\frac{\pi}{4}(D_C+D_R)(D_C-D_R)L
VD=π4(DC+DR)(DC−DR)LV_D=\frac{\pi}{4}(D_C+D_R)(D_C-D_R)LVD​=4π​(DC​+DR​)(DC​−DR​)L

substituting eeee, we obtain:

4.1.5 Piston Pumps

Piston pumps consist of a cylinder block containing multiple pistons arranged in a circular pattern. As the pump operates, a swash plate or cam mechanism drives the pistons in and out of their cylinders. This reciprocating motion creates alternating suction and discharge as the pistons draw in and expel fluid.

In some designs, the angle of the swash plate can be adjusted, changing the stroke length of the pistons. This variation controls the displacement, creating a variable displacement piston pump. Other designs, such as bent-axis pumps, have a set angle and thus a set volumetric displacement.

The volumetric displacement of a piston pump is thus the number of pistons mutiplied by the displacement volume per piston. The stroke of the piston, s, is offset at an angle, \thetaθ\thetaθ. As the pistons are set about a piston circle, the piston circle diameter (i.e. the circle the pistons are arranged around), D and the offset angle determine the effective stroke length of the piston.

By trigonometric analysis,
tan(\theta) = s/Dtan(θ)=s/Dtan(\theta) = s/Dtan(θ)=s/D
and
s = D \times tan(\theta)s=D×tan(θ)s = D \times tan(\theta)s=D×tan(θ)

Using this relation, we end up with the following equation for volumetric displacement:

V_D=YADtan(\theta)
VD=YADtan(θ)V_D=YADtan(\theta)VD​=YADtan(θ)

where Y is the number of pistons, and A is the piston area.

4.1.6 Efficiency

There is bound to be leakage between the gear and the pump housing as there needs to be clearance to allow for movement. The pump performance after accounting for leakage is called volumetric efficiency and is given by the formula:

\eta_v=\frac{Q_A}{Q_T}
ηv=QAQT\eta_v=\frac{Q_A}{Q_T}ηv​=QT​QA​​

Q_AQAQ_AQA​ is actual flowrate which is determined by testing, and Q_TQTQ_TQT​ is theoretical flowrate which is calculated in 4.1.1.

As components in the hydraulic system push, pull, and contact each other, friction causes energy loss. Fluid turbulence also contributes to energy loss. The output power accounting for these losses is called mechanical efficiency and is given by the formula:

\eta_m=\frac{PQ_T}{T_AN}
ηm=PQTTAN\eta_m=\frac{PQ_T}{T_AN}ηm​=TA​NPQT​​

where T_ATAT_ATA​ is the torque delivered to the pump, and N is the pump speed in rad/srad/srad/srad/s. This frames mechanical efficiency as pump output power divided by power delivered to the pump.

Another method to describe mechanical efficiency is to divide the torque theoretically required to drive the pump with the torque actually being drawn, shown as:

\eta_m=\frac{T_T}{T_A}
ηm=TTTA\eta_m=\frac{T_T}{T_A}ηm​=TA​TT​​

By putting both efficiencies, together, we can obtain the overall efficiency, or the power delivered by the pump divided by the power delivered to the pump

\eta_o=\eta_v \times \eta_m=\frac{Q_A}{Q_T} \times \frac{PQ_T}{T_AN}= \frac{PQ_A}{T_AN}

4.2 Hydraulic Actuators

Hydraulic actuators fall into two general categories; linear and rotary. Cylinders provide linear motion, and motors provide rotary motion.

Types of cylinder construction

  1. Tie-rod
  2. Bolted
  3. Welded

Types of cylinder mounting

  1. Clevis mount: A fork-shaped connection with a pin for easy attachment to a fixed point.
  2. Flange mount
    1. Front flange: Attached to the cylinder's front with bolts for secure mounting.
    2. Rear flange: Mounted at the rear, often with bolts to connect to a machine.
  3. Trunnion mount: A cylindrical pin connection allowing rotation around a central axis.
  4. Foot mount: Flat, bolted base providing stable mounting for the cylinder.
