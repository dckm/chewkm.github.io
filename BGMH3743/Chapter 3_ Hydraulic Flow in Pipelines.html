3.1 Flow in Pipelines

Flow of fluids in an enclosed channel is a very common application of fluid mechanics. In hydraulics, our main concern is the loss of energy due to friction in flow.

We have already seen that the velocity of a flow of fluid follows a velocity profile i.e. the fluid flows faster the further away from the wall it is. It follows that layers of flow exist which pull against each other, producing drag.

When the layers flow smoothly with each other, the flow remains laminar. It is smooth with little mixing between layers. As the drag increases, the layers collide and cause turbulent flow to appear. Turbulent flow causes large amounts of friction and energy loss.

3.2 Laminar and Turbulent Flow

Laminar and turbulent flow was characterised by Osbourne Reynolds, who conducted experiments and developed a formula to calculate Reynolds number, which is used to predict the flow regime of fluid in a channel.

After Reynolds' work, the Darcy-Weisbach equation incorporated a friction factor to estimate the pressure loss in pipes due to friction.

Lewis Moody created the Moody Diagram by plotting the friction factor, Reynolds number, and relative pipe roughness, which can be used to predict pressure drop or flow rate.

3.3 Pressure loss

Pressure loss is an important factor in the design of hydraulic power systems. As fluid flows though pipes and components, turbulence and bends will reduce the energy in the flow, lowering its pressure. As shown at the end of Chapter 2, pressure directly affects the power of the system. Therefore, we are very interested in knowing what the pressure will be at certain points of the system, e.g. at the output cylinder.

In hydraulics, pressure loss (Pa) is often expressed in terms of head loss (m), the concept being that a particular height of fluid exerts a corresponding amount of pressure. By expressing loss in metres, it becomes easier to compare systems and fluids by using a unit independent of density.

3.4 Losses in fittings and valves

So far, we have only considered frictional losses in pipes. However, hydraulic systems require fittings and valves to control direction, pressure, and flow rate. These also introduce losses which may be greater than the losses in pipes.

Some examples of valves and fittings are:

  • globe valve
  • gate valve
  • check valve
  • 45° elbow
  • straight tee

Losses in fittings and valves vary based on its construction. The less the component restricts the flow of fluid, the lower the energy loss. This energy loss is called the K-factor and is proportional to the fluid velocity. Many components have been characterised by experimentation and assigned a K-factor.
